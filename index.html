Study Notes

Nature of Economics

  • Economics as social science: Studies human behavior in allocating scarce resources
  • Positive vs Normative statements: Positive = factual/testable; Normative = opinion-based/value judgements
  • Scarcity & Opportunity Cost: Limited resources require choices; opportunity cost = value of next best alternative forgone
  • PPF Diagrams: Shows maximum production combinations; shifts = change in productive capacity; movements = change in allocation
  • Specialisation/Division of Labour: Increases efficiency but creates interdependence
  • Functions of Money: Medium of exchange, store of value, unit of account, standard of deferred payment
  • Economy Types: Free market (price mechanism), Mixed (public + private), Command (central planning)

How Markets Work

  • Rational Decision Making: Consumers maximize utility; producers maximize profit
  • Demand Determinants: Price, income, tastes, substitutes/complements, population, expectations
  • Supply Determinants: Costs of production, technology, subsidies/taxes, expectations, number of suppliers
  • Market Equilibrium: Where demand = supply; consumer + producer surplus maximized
  • Elasticities:
    • PED = % change Qd / % change P
    • PES = % change Qs / % change P
    • YED = % change Qd / % change Y
    • XED = % change Qd good A / % change P good B
  • Price Mechanism Functions: Signaling (information), Incentivising (rewards), Rationing (allocation)
  • Alternative Behaviors: Bounded rationality, herding behavior, behavioral economics

Market Failure

  • Externalities:
    • Negative: Third-party costs (pollution) → over-consumption/production
    • Positive: Third-party benefits (education) → under-consumption/production
  • Public Goods: Non-excludable + non-rival → free rider problem → under-provision
  • Information Gaps: Asymmetric information → poor decisions
    • Merit goods: Under-consumed (healthcare, education)
    • Demerit goods: Over-consumed (tobacco, alcohol)
  • Welfare Loss: Deadweight loss from market failure shown on diagrams
  • Government Failure: Regulatory capture, unintended consequences, bureaucracy costs

Government Intervention

  • Taxes: Direct (income) vs Indirect (VAT); reduce consumption/production
  • Subsidies: Increase consumption/production; reduce market price
  • Price Controls:
    • Minimum prices (above equilibrium) → surpluses
    • Maximum prices (below equilibrium) → shortages
  • Regulation: Rules/standards; tradeable permits for pollution
  • Real Example: UK sugar tax on soft drinks - reduced consumption by 30%
  • Evaluation: Effectiveness depends on elasticity, enforcement, side effects

Measures of Economic Performance

  • Real GDP: Value of goods/services adjusted for inflation; measures economic size
  • Inflation: CPI (basket of goods) vs RPI (includes housing); target = 2%
  • Unemployment Types:
    • Cyclical: Due to economic downturn
    • Structural: Skills mismatch from economic change
    • Frictional: Job searching time
    • Seasonal: Time of year variations
  • Balance of Payments: Current account (trade) + Capital account (investment flows)
  • GDP Limitations: Doesn't include unpaid work, environmental costs, income distribution, shadow economy

Aggregate Demand (AD)

  • AD Formula: C + I + G + (X-M)
  • Consumption (C): Influenced by income, confidence, interest rates, wealth
  • Investment (I): Influenced by interest rates, confidence, technology, regulations
  • Government Spending (G): Policy decision based on economic objectives
  • Net Exports (X-M): Exchange rates, relative inflation, income abroad
  • MPC/MPS: Marginal propensity to consume/save from additional income
  • Multiplier Effect: Initial injection → rounds of spending → larger final impact
  • Multiplier = 1/(1-MPC) = 1/MPS

Aggregate Supply (AS)

  • SRAS Factors: Wages, raw material costs, productivity, indirect taxes/subsidies
  • LRAS Factors: Technology, education/training, infrastructure, competition, supply shocks
  • Supply Shocks: Sudden changes in costs (oil prices, weather)
  • Productivity: Output per worker; key for long-term growth

National Income & Economic Growth

  • Circular Flow: Households ↔ Firms with leakages (S,T,M) and injections (I,G,X)
  • Equilibrium: When S+T+M = I+G+X
  • Actual Growth: Increase in real GDP (movement towards PPF)
  • Potential Growth: Increase in productive capacity (PPF shift outward)
  • Output Gaps: Positive (above trend) = inflation pressure; Negative (below trend) = unemployment
  • Benefits: Higher living standards, employment, tax revenue
  • Costs: Environmental damage, inequality, inflation

Macroeconomic Policies

  • Objectives: Economic growth, low inflation, low unemployment, balanced BOP
  • Fiscal Policy: Government spending and taxation
    • Expansionary: Increase G, cut taxes
    • Contractionary: Cut G, raise taxes
  • Monetary Policy: Interest rates, money supply, QE
    • Lower rates = stimulate demand
    • Higher rates = reduce inflation
  • Supply-Side Policies: Education, training, deregulation, infrastructure
  • Policy Conflicts: Growth vs inflation, unemployment vs inflation (Phillips curve)
  • Time Lags: Recognition, implementation, impact lags affect effectiveness

Exam Strategy & Techniques

KAAE Method

Essential approach for high-mark questions (4+ marks):

  • Knowledge: Start with clear definitions and economic terms
  • Application: Use data from the question or real-world examples
  • Analysis: Develop chains of reasoning with economic diagrams
  • Evaluation: Make balanced judgements with counterarguments

Essay Writing Technique

  1. Brief Introduction: Define key terms and outline your approach
  2. Analysis Paragraphs (2-3): Each with diagrams and chains of reasoning
  3. Evaluation Paragraphs (2-3): Consider limitations, alternatives, context
  4. Clear Conclusion: Justified judgement based on your analysis
  5. Contemporary Examples: Use recent UK economic events and data

Exam Structure (1hr 30min, 80 marks)

  • Section A: Multiple choice & short answers (1-3 marks) - 15 minutes
  • Section B: Medium answers (4-15 marks) requiring analysis and diagrams - 45 minutes
  • Section C: One long essay (up to 20 marks) with evaluation - 30 minutes

Key Diagrams to Master

  • Supply and Demand with shifts and movements
  • Consumer and Producer Surplus
  • Externality diagrams (positive and negative)
  • Tax and subsidy effects on markets
  • Minimum and maximum price controls
  • AD/AS model with policy impacts
  • Multiplier effect illustration
  • Output gaps on AD/AS diagram
  • Phillips curve relationship

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Not reading the question carefully - identify command words
  • Providing description instead of analysis
  • Missing evaluation in higher mark questions
  • Drawing diagrams without proper labeling
  • Not using data or examples when available
  • Running out of time on essay questions
